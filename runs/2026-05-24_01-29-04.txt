MITIGATION PLAYBOOK
==================================================

Alternate Supplier Actions
--------------------------
1. For Taiwan advanced logic and mature-node exposure, identify and qualify backup wafer capacity outside Taiwan where design rules allow: Samsung Foundry and Intel Foundry for advanced logic, GlobalFoundries/UMC Singapore/SMIC where compliant for mature nodes, and Japan Advanced Semiconductor Manufacturing for eligible automotive and industrial wafers.
2. For gallium, germanium, GaAs, GaN, SiGe, RF, photonic, and power semiconductor materials, dual-source with non-China or China-plus-one suppliers such as Umicore, 5N Plus, Freiberger Compound Materials, Sumitomo Electric Industries, AXT non-China channels, and qualified recycling/reclaim providers; pre-clear export-control documentation with suppliers before purchase orders are placed.
3. For HBM, CoWoS, advanced packaging, and OSAT constraints, secure second-source packaging and test routes through ASE, Amkor, Samsung, Intel Foundry Services packaging, and qualified substrate/interposer suppliers; prioritize qualification of alternate package designs or lower-HBM configurations for products exposed to SK hynix, Samsung, Micron, and TSMC CoWoS allocation.

Inventory Actions
-----------------
1. Immediately raise safety stock targets for Taiwan-sourced advanced logic wafers, automotive/industrial mature-node wafers, DRAM, power management ICs, microcontrollers, and critical packaged ICs to cover at least the expected recovery window for earthquake inspections, water rationing, or fab tool recalibration delays.
2. Create ring-fenced buffer stock for gallium, germanium, GaAs wafers, GaN devices, HBM3/HBM3E, AI accelerator substrates, 2.5D interposers, photoresists, etching gases, and high-purity process chemicals; prevent consumption by non-critical builds without supply-chain leadership approval.
3. Pull forward purchase orders and supplier capacity reservations for long-lead semiconductors and manufacturing equipment spares affected by U.S.-China controls, Red Sea routing delays, and Korea logistics disruption; allocate constrained components first to revenue-critical, automotive safety, customer-committed, and single-sourced programs.

Logistics Actions
-----------------
1. Move high-value chips, HBM, photoresists, process chemicals where permitted, fab tool spares, and urgent engineering samples from ocean to air freight on Asia-Europe and Korea/Taiwan-Europe lanes while Red Sea diversions persist; update planning lead times for ocean shipments routed via the Cape of Good Hope.
2. Pre-book alternate port and inland routes for South Korea and Taiwan exposure: use Incheon, Pyeongtaek, Gwangyang, and air cargo alternatives if Busan trucking is disrupted; secure bonded warehouse capacity near fabs and airports for DRAM, NAND, chemicals, and semiconductor equipment spares.
3. For Malaysia and Vietnam back-end operations, prioritize shipments from OSAT sites with stable power and available generator-backed capacity; split lots across Malaysia, Vietnam, Taiwan, Singapore, and the Philippines where qualified to avoid finished-chip delays from localized power instability.

Communication Actions
---------------------
1. Establish a daily semiconductor disruption war room including procurement, supply planning, engineering, logistics, legal/export compliance, quality, operations, and executive leadership to review Taiwan water/seismic status, export-control changes, HBM/CoWoS allocation, Korea trucking, and cyber incidents.
2. Send targeted supplier questionnaires to TSMC, UMC, Samsung, SK hynix, Micron, ASE, Amkor, ASML, Infineon, STMicroelectronics, NXP, Renesas, Sony Semiconductor Solutions, and key materials suppliers requesting current capacity, allocation status, water and power contingency plans, shipment backlogs, and recovery-time estimates.
3. Provide customer and stakeholder updates for products dependent on Taiwan fabs, HBM, advanced packaging, Korea memory, gallium/germanium materials, and Southeast Asia OSAT capacity; communicate confirmed impacts, inventory protection actions, allocation rules, and any lead-time or delivery-date changes.
